\chapter{Information-satisfaction evaluation of query-based summarization systems} \label{chap:chap-7}


\section*{Non-technical Abstract}
When computers automatically summarize documents, how do we know if the summaries are any good? Researchers have built various tools to measure summary quality, from older word-matching methods to newer approaches that use AI systems as judges. However, these metrics fail to acknowledge a good summary for one person isn't necessarily good for another. A scientist studying vaccine development needs different details than a family doctor reading about the same research. In this chapter, we tested whether popular evaluation tools can detect meaningful changes in a summary's information content, and we asked human experts to judge summaries based on whether the information matched what a specific reader would actually need. Both the traditional metrics and the newer AI-based ones fell short, revealing a real gap between what we're measuring and what readers actually care about.
